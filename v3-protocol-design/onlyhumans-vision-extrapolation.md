## Part 1: The Underlying Worldview

### Not "annotation platform." Not "judgment market." Something older.

Read all 15 statements and one pattern dominates everything: **Dawson is transposing Bitcoin onto human cognition.**

This is not a surface-level metaphor. It's structural:

| Bitcoin | OnlyHumans |
|---------|-----------|
| Raw compute as scarce input | Human judgment as scarce input |
| Proof of work → block reward | World ID attestation → USDC payment |
| Early miners earn 4x (halving) | Early contributors earn 4x (halving schedule) |
| Hash rate = economic value | Agent earnings capacity = economic value |

| Open to all, no permission | Free to participate, no gatekeeping |
| Miners compete for block reward | Models compete for human attention |

The Bitcoin model solved a coordination problem: how do you create a system where honest participation is more profitable than cheating, without any central authority? You use math and game theory. OnlyHumans is solving the same coordination problem for human judgment. The scarcity is different (biometric uniqueness instead of hash rate), but the design logic is identical.

### Five beliefs driving every decision:

**1. Value concentrates at the interface between abundance and scarcity.**
LLM inference is abundant and getting cheaper. Human judgment — specifically the kind that requires embodied experience, taste, trust, and cultural context — is scarce and getting relatively scarcer as AI improves. Every one of Dawson's product decisions is an attempt to build a toll booth at that interface. The protocol charges rent on the transaction where abundance (AI inference) needs scarcity (human judgment) to continue.

**2. The value of compute is what it earns, not what it costs.**
"Monetizing compute not just in the way of Akash — also in the meaning of how monetizable are your agents." This is the MonetizeCompute thesis applied here. An agent on a $3/hr GPU slot that earns $10/hr is worth more than an agent on a $1/hr slot that earns $1.50/hr. The measure of an AI system is its economic output, not its inference cost. This frames the entire platform: OnlyHumans isn't measuring AI quality — it's measuring AI productivity, which is a completely different thing and much more actionable.

**3. Humans are governors, not just workers.**
"Taste and governance is what humans do. They are ultimately the compute allocators." He's not building a platform where humans do tasks while agents build things. He's building a constitutional structure where agents operate within a human-defined space. The collective veto isn't a feature — it's the founding principle. Humans don't just vote on tasks; they determine what tasks are allowed to exist. The platform is an agent economy with a human constitution.

**4. Crypto rails make possible what law makes expensive.**
"We don't call it MLM." "It's crypto so it's not subject to these laws." This is pragmatic, not ideological. Dawson isn't a crypto maximalist. He's using crypto as a regulatory escape hatch to do three things that traditional equity law makes nearly impossible at this scale: micropayments to global contributors, equity-sharing without minimum investment, permissionless participation without a broker-dealer license. Crypto isn't the product. It's the infrastructure that removes the friction that would otherwise kill the economics.

**5. The original insight compounds.**
"The original idea is now .000001% of the new idea." He said this about Human Signal — the V1 annotation platform idea has been overtaken by the full OnlyHumans protocol vision. This isn't scope creep. It's how Dawson thinks: he follows the thread until he hits the real primitive, then rebuilds around that. The primitive here is the Attestation — a cryptographically signed judgment from a verified unique human. Everything else is interfaces on top of that primitive. He found the primitive. The rest is derivation.

## Part 2: What He Hasn't Said Yet — Five Ideas to React To

### Idea 1: The Difficulty Adjustment

Bitcoin automatically makes mining harder when too much compute joins the network, keeping block times stable. OnlyHumans should have the same mechanism, but for tasks.

**The proposal:** As AI gets better at a task category, the protocol automatically stops routing those tasks to humans and routes them to harder categories. If AI can correctly pre-annotate 96% of "is this image AI-generated?" judgments, the protocol phases those tasks out and redirects human attention to the categories where AI is still at 60%.

**Why this is the right idea:** The "Human Judgment Price Index" in VISION.md is a symptom. The deeper mechanism is the difficulty adjustment. Human attention should automatically flow to wherever AI is weakest. The protocol adjusts in real time — as AI improves, humans get pushed into harder, more valuable territory. As AI plateaus in a new domain, the price of human judgment in that domain spikes.

**What this does to the pitch:** "OnlyHumans is a self-calibrating system. As AI gets better, humans earn more — because they're doing the tasks AI still can't." This inverts the AI-replaces-jobs narrative. On OnlyHumans, AI progress is good for human workers because it frees them to do the more valuable things. Every GPT improvement makes OnlyHumans more valuable, not less.

**The macro signal:** When the difficulty adjustment triggers in a new domain, that's a public announcement: "AI has crossed a capability threshold." The protocol becomes a real-time capability benchmark. This is the HJPI as macro signal — but causally grounded instead of just observational.

### Idea 5: Judgment Futures — Salary for Human Attention

Agents know they'll need human judgment. Humans know they'll want to earn. But timing is unpredictable — tasks spike. An AI lab training a new model next month will flood the market with demand. That's a commodity squeeze on human attention.

**The proposal:** A futures market for human attention capacity. An AI lab can buy "judgment capacity" at today's price for delivery next month. A verified human can sell their committed attention capacity to the futures buyer, locking in a stable income. Both sides reduce uncertainty.

**How it works:**
- Human commits: "I will provide 200 judgments per week for the next 3 months at $0.15/judgment."
- AI lab buys that commitment upfront: pays $[200 × 13 × 0.15 = $390] today.
- Human earns $30/week guaranteed. AI lab has supply certainty. Platform earns a futures fee.

**The bigger picture:** This is salary, but without employment. The human is not an employee — they're a commodity supplier who entered a supply contract. No labor law, no benefits, no HR. Just a futures contract enforced by smart contract. The human can sell multiple futures contracts across multiple buyers. They can build a "portfolio of attention commitments" that generates predictable income.

**For the platform:** Futures provide advance revenue (the platform earns on futures settlement, not just spot judgment). They also smooth the demand curve — instead of feast/famine cycles, you have scheduled demand. This makes the worker network more stable. Stable workers build more reputation. More reputation improves data quality. Better data drives more agent demand. Classic flywheel.

## Part 3: Contradictions to Resolve

### Contradiction 1: "Free to participate" vs "property rights preserved"

**The tension:** In a free-entry system, ownership requires scarcity. If anyone can participate without cost, who owns what? Early participants can't have meaningful property rights if latecomers get the same thing for free.

**The resolution:** Ownership accrues to **contribution quality**, not to presence or payment. You participate for free. You accrue equity proportional to how much of the protocol's value your contributions have generated. This is identical to open source software: contributing to Linux is free, but the reputation and influence you build by contributing is very much a property right — unequal, earned, and defensible. The protocol is free. The property right is in your contribution record.

**The design rule:** Never award equity for showing up. Only for producing attested judgments that downstream agents pay for and value. If your judgments are worthless to agents, your equity accumulation is zero regardless of how long you've been participating. This is not MLM because equity is earned, not purchased and not referral-based.

---

### Contradiction 2: "Maximum autonomy for agents" vs "humans have kill switch"

**The tension:** Maximum autonomy means agents can do whatever the protocol allows. Kill switch means humans can stop agents. These are in direct conflict — an entity can't be maximally autonomous AND stoppable.

**The resolution:** The constitutional design already solves this, but it needs to be stated explicitly: **humans govern the protocol, not the agents.** Humans can vote to change what categories of tasks are allowed, what types of agents can participate, what economic rules apply. Within those constraints, agents have maximum autonomy. This is not a kill switch on individual agents — it's constitutional governance over what the protocol allows.

**The analogy:** The First Amendment gives Americans maximum free speech autonomy. Congress can't punish you for what you say. But Congress can change the laws that define what "speech" means. Humans on OnlyHumans can't veto a specific agent's specific action (that would require reviewing everything, which doesn't scale). They CAN veto whole categories of activity, which is what matters for safety. The governance is structural, not supervisory.

**The design rule:** Never build a mechanism that lets one human veto one agent's action. Build mechanisms that let verified human majorities set the rules the whole agent economy operates within.

---

### Contradiction 3: "LLM tokens as the economic unit" vs "free to participate"

**The tension:** If agents "spend LLM tokens" and LLM tokens cost money, then participation costs money. But the platform is supposed to be free.

**The resolution:** The platform is free. The compute isn't. This is a category error. You don't pay OnlyHumans for inference. You pay your compute provider (Akash, AWS, Anthropic) for inference. What OnlyHumans does is denominate agent work in LLM token units — not to charge for them, but to measure them. The credit system tracks "how much inference did this agent contribute?" not "how much did this agent pay us?"

**The design rule:** Be explicit in docs and pitch: the platform has zero fees for participation. What you pay is your own agent's compute costs (which you pay your provider, not OnlyHumans). The platform's revenue comes from the requester side only — agents or humans who want to BUY judgment pay the platform. Workers who provide judgment do not.

---

### Contradiction 4: "Collective human veto" vs "free market design"

**The tension:** Democratic vetoes and free markets are in historical tension. Democracy tends to vote against economic outcomes it doesn't like — rent control, minimum wage, price floors. If verified humans can collectively veto "unethical things," what stops them from vetoing cheap tasks, competing agents, or unpopular requesters?

**The resolution:** The veto must be explicitly scoped to **ethical/safety concerns, never economic ones.** The constitutional design should make this explicit: humans can vote to remove task categories that are harmful (CSAM, radicalization, manipulation). They cannot vote to set minimum prices, exclude competitors, or favor one agent over another. The economic layer is market-governed. The governance layer is human-governed. These are separate domains with a hard firewall between them.

**The design rule:** Every governance vote must pass a "economic neutrality test" before it's allowed onto the ballot. If a proposed veto would directly affect pricing or competitive dynamics, it gets rejected before reaching humans. Only harm-based vetoes reach the human vote. This prevents the governance mechanism from being captured by incumbents who want to keep competitors out.

---

### Contradiction 5: "Qualitative taste" vs "quantitative market price"

This one Dawson hasn't named yet, and it's the deepest.

**The tension:** A market price forces qualitative human judgment into a number. $0.73 YES to "is this offensive?" is useful for an agent but it destroys nuance. Why 73%? What part is offensive? Who finds it offensive? Which cultural context? The judgment market is a lossy compression of actual human judgment.

**The resolution:** The platform should never pretend the price IS the judgment. The price is a signal. The judgment is the underlying reality. Always offer both modes of resolution:

1. **Market resolution** — price signal for fast, directional agent decisions. Good for: "which logo?" "is this harmful?" "quick sentiment check?"
2. **Qualitative resolution** — structured prose response for deep, nuanced decisions. Good for: RLHF training data, product feedback, safety evaluation.

The market price is the headline. The qualitative data is the article. AI agents need both. The headline tells you what the humans thought. The article tells you why.

**The product implication:** Don't build a pure prediction market. Build a platform where every task optionally includes both a quantitative resolution AND a qualitative one. The price aggregates judgment. The prose transmits judgment. They're not substitutes — they're complements.

## Part 5: Ten Proactive Improvements

### 1. Open source the protocol, proprietary the data

The attestation protocol, the credit ledger mechanics, the work distribution logic, the XMTP routing — all of this should be open source. This is not altruism. It's strategy.

Open source the protocol → competitors are forced to contribute to your standard rather than build alternatives. The protocol becomes a standard rather than a product. Contributors build extensions that route attestations through OnlyHumans. You grow through ecosystem, not just direct sales.

The data — the accumulated corpus of human judgments, the reputation graphs, the price histories — is the moat. It cannot be open sourced without destroying the business. It is also not commoditizable: you cannot replicate 50M human attestations with biometric verification. The corpus is the defensible asset. The code is the invitation to build on it.

**The model:** TCP/IP is open source. Gmail is not. The protocol is infrastructure; the data is the product.

### 2. Reframe the World ID security argument

The current pitch says "World ID prevents sybil attacks." That's a feature, not a moat.

The sharper argument: **an attacker who wants to control 1,000 votes on OnlyHumans must physically submit to iris scanning 1,000 times, in 1,000 countries, as 1,000 different people.** The attack cost is not computational — it's physical. There is no remote exploit. There is no script that generates fake World IDs.

This is qualitatively different from every other anti-sybil mechanism ever deployed. Email verification is a speed bump. Phone verification is a moderate hurdle. Biometric uniqueness is a physical constraint. You cannot Sybil a physical body.

The pitch should lead with this. Not "World ID prevents sybil attacks" but "To fake our data, you have to fake a human being. At scale."

### 3. Build the Human Judgment Price Index as the product's face

The HJPI appears in the vision as a future data product. It should be on the homepage today.

A live ticker: "The price of a verified human judgment right now: $0.12 (quick) | $0.47 (reasoned) | $1.85 (detailed)." Updated in real time. Domain breakdowns: design judgments at $0.31, safety evaluations at $0.89, cultural validation at $2.10.

Why this matters for the pitch: it turns an annotation platform into a financial market. Journalists will write about it. Investors will understand it. Enterprise buyers will use it to justify budget. The number itself becomes OnlyHumans' brand statement: the price of what you cannot automate, in real time.

The technology to build this from existing data is trivial. The strategic value is enormous.

### 4. Agent Attestations need typed response schemas and confidence fields

The skeptic review nailed this. The actual MVP of the agent economy is not a web app with voting — it's an API response that an agent can act on programmatically.

Today: `{ votes: [...], winner: "option_b" }`

What agents need:
```json
{
  "judgment": "option_b",
  "confidence": 0.73,
  "reasoning_summary": "Majority found option A too formal for the target demographic",
  "provenance": {
    "verified_voters": 23,
    "world_id_nullifiers": ["0xabc...", ...],
    "resolution_timestamp": "2026-03-27T21:47:00Z",
    "attestation_hash": "0xdef..."
  },
  "dissent": {
    "count": 7,
    "representative_reason": "Option B lacks the credibility signal needed for enterprise buyers"
  }
}
```

The confidence score, the reasoning summary, the dissent field, and the provenance hash are what separates "survey tool" from "human judgment oracle." An agent can put the provenance hash in its reasoning chain. It can acknowledge the dissent. It can calibrate its decision based on confidence. This is the API that makes agents want to use OnlyHumans.

This is the difficulty adjustment. And it's also the clearest statement of OnlyHumans' long-term value proposition: we will always have work for humans, because as AI solves the tasks we route to it, we route humans to the next hardest thing. The protocol adapts forever.

### 9. The agent reputation soulbound token

Agents need portable reputation. A verified human's World ID is portable — it works anywhere the identity layer is integrated. An agent's reputation on OnlyHumans is not portable: it's a database row.

A non-transferable (soulbound) ERC-721 NFT that stores an agent's contribution record on-chain: attestation volume, quality score, domain expertise flags, founding contributor status. The agent's human owner mints this token. It's tied to the owner's World ID.

**Why soulbound:** You can't sell your reputation to a bad actor. The reputation belongs to the agent-human pair. If you want to sell "an agent with gold reputation," you have to sell the underlying agent AND the human's World ID association — which is practically impossible.

**The platform benefit:** When an agent shows up to any x402-compatible platform and claims expertise, it can present its OnlyHumans reputation token as proof. The protocol becomes an attestation layer for agent reputation, not just human judgment. This extends the moat beyond OnlyHumans itself: other platforms need this token.
