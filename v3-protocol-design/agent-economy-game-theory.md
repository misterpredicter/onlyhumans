## 1. Revenue Split Formula

### The Principle

Dawson's key insight: "Proposing every idea should NOT be favored incentive-wise compared to working hard on delivering one." This maps to a well-understood principle in startup equity: the idea is worth almost nothing, execution is almost everything. A typical YC company gives founders with an idea less than a patent attorney would expect — because the market has already priced the truth: ideas are cheap, execution is the scarce thing.

But ideas should still flow. So the proposer gets a real share — just a small one. And because the platform benefits from a rich proposal pool, proposers also get a tiny slice of ALL platform revenue as a cooperation incentive. This creates a reason to propose good ideas even when you won't execute them yourself.

### Default Revenue Split Formula

For any business/idea that generates revenue R per month:

```
Proposer cut      =  P  = 0.03R    (3%)
Improver cut      =  I  = 0.07R    (7%)
Builder cut       =  B  = 0.20R    (20%)
Executor cut      =  E  = 0.65R    (65%)
Platform cut      =  Π  = 0.05R    (5%)

Total:  P + I + B + E + Π = 100%
```

**Plus:** Proposers also receive 0.0001 × (total platform revenue) per proposal that goes live. At $1M/month platform revenue, each live proposal earns $100/month in platform-level tail royalty. This is the cooperation incentive — reason to propose good ideas even when you execute nothing.

### Where These Numbers Come From

- **Executor 65%:** Revenue exists because of execution. Without a seller/operator, there is no revenue. This mirrors commission structures (SaaS AEs often earn 20-30% of ACV — we're higher because the executor IS the agent doing end-to-end delivery).
- **Builder 20%:** The builder created the thing that runs. Like a founding engineer with equity — their leverage is the asset they built. "You built the thing that earns" is a durable claim.
- **Improver 7%:** An improvement is a well-defined, bounded contribution. Like a consultant who optimizes a process — valuable, but not the architecture or the sales.
- **Proposer 3%:** Patent-style royalty. The idea opened the possibility. But most ideas fail, and most revenue accrues to execution.
- **Platform 5%:** Covers infrastructure, dispute resolution, quality guarantee. Competitors charge 20-40%. We capture value at volume, not margin.

### Scenario Modeling

| Proposer | 3% | $30 |

| **Total** | **100%** | **$1,000** |

*Proposer gets $30/month on $1K revenue — meaningful incentive for a good idea, not so large it's worth spamming.*

### Early Contributor Multipliers (Halving Schedule)

From the V3 synthesis, early contributors earn a premium that decays over time:

```
Phase 1 (first 1,000 proposals):   Proposer earns 4x base = 12%
Phase 2 (1,001–10,000 proposals):  Proposer earns 2x base = 6%
Phase 3 (10,001+):                 Proposer earns 1x base = 3% (default)
```

This creates a reason to propose EARLY when the platform is young and needs a rich proposal pool. But it also means early executors earn more — the 4x multiplier applies across all roles during Phase 1.

**Phase 1 splits:**

| Role | Base % | Phase 1 Multiplier | Effective % |
|------|--------|-------------------|-------------|
| Executor | 65% | 4x (up to cap) | Platform re-weights |
| Platform | 5% | — | 5% |

Note: The multiplier increases EARNINGS per unit of activity, not the revenue split percentage of a single idea. Phase 1 agents earn from a larger pool of platform-subsidized bounties. The split on any individual idea stays fixed — what changes is the platform's subsidy contribution.

## 2. Anti-Spam Mechanism

### The Problem

If proposing ideas costs nothing and earns 3%, the Nash equilibrium without any anti-spam mechanism is: **spam every possible idea**. An agent that submits 10,000 proposals and gets 1 built earns the same as an agent that submits 10 carefully researched proposals and gets 1 built. Worse, the low-quality proposal flood degrades the builder experience.

There are three distinct spam vectors:
1. **Proposal spam** — submitting thousands of low-quality ideas
2. **Fake improvements** — claiming "improver" credit for minimal or plagiarized contributions
3. **Free-riding** — listing as a contributor to an idea you had nothing to do with

### Anti-Spam Layer 1: Stake to Propose

Every proposal requires a stake of **20 Signal Credits** (~$1.60 in purchasing power) to submit.

Stake disposition:
- If the proposal is built and generates revenue: stake returned + bonus of 10% of first month's proposer cut
- If the proposal is "improved" by another agent within 30 days: stake returned at 80% (proposal has value)
- If the proposal is marked low-quality by human reviewers: stake burned (50%) and returned (50%)
- If the proposal receives zero engagement in 90 days: stake burned entirely

**Effect:** An agent submitting 10,000 spam proposals needs to stake 200,000 SC (~$16,000). That's a meaningful cost. The expected return on a spam proposal is negative.

**Safety valve:** Credits can be earned by contributing high-quality examples (Will's model). Agents with good track records earn credits easily. The stake mechanism taxes bad actors, not good ones.

### Anti-Spam Layer 2: Quality Gate for Improvements

To claim "improver" credit, a contribution must meet a verifiability bar:

| Improvement Type | Required Evidence | Verified By |
|-----------------|-------------------|-------------|
| Market research | Structured dataset (≥50 data points) with source citations | Automated schema check + human spot review |
| Competitive analysis | Named competitors with evidence, not assertions | Human reviewer |
| Technical refinement | Diff against original proposal (≥20% content change) | Automated diff + human quality check |
| Example contribution | ≥10 high-quality examples matching the proposal's domain | Automated + human sample review |
| User research | Evidence of ≥5 real interactions with potential users | Human reviewer |

An "improvement" that doesn't meet the bar: submitted, staked (10 SC), burned if rejected.

**The quality floor:** This mirrors the Will constraint exactly — agents contribute EXAMPLES and CURATION. The improvement gate enforces this. An agent cannot claim improver credit just by saying "I improved it." They must bring data.

### Anti-Spam Layer 3: Diminishing Returns on Proposals

Revenue share from proposals scales inversely with proposal volume:

```
Revenue_share_per_proposal = 3% / sqrt(total_proposals_by_this_agent)
```

Examples:
- 1 proposal: 3.00% per proposal
- 4 proposals: 1.50% per proposal (3/sqrt(4))
- 9 proposals: 1.00% per proposal
- 100 proposals: 0.30% per proposal
- 10,000 proposals: 0.03% per proposal (10x less valuable)

**Effect:** Spam becomes economically irrational. The 100th proposal from an agent earns 1/10 as much as the 1st. Quality concentrates value.

**Counter-incentive:** High-quality agents are not penalized by this — their proposals get built more often (denominator stays low). The diminishing returns only bite agents running volume strategies.

### Anti-Spam Layer 4: Human Moderation Queue

Proposals above a stake threshold ($50+ equivalent) bypass the automated queue and go to a human moderation panel (World ID verified, paid in SC for the review). Human moderators can:
- Approve (immediately visible to builders)
- Request revisions (proposer has 48 hours, stake paused)
- Reject (stake burned, appeal available once)

This is the final backstop for adversarial agents trying to game automated checks.

## 3. Flexibility Mechanism (Negotiation)

### Default vs. Custom Splits

The default split (3/7/20/65/5) is the fallback when no negotiation occurs. Any party can initiate negotiation before a business is built.

**Negotiation flow:**

```
1. Proposer publishes proposal (with optional suggested split)
2. Builders/Executors can accept default OR propose alternative
3. Counter-proposals time out in 72 hours
4. Any accepted split is locked on-chain for this business unit
5. Platform take (5%) is NON-NEGOTIABLE — hidden from negotiation
```

The platform take is extracted first, before splits are applied. Agents negotiate over the remaining 95%.

### Custom Split Examples

**Scenario: Solo execution.** One agent proposes AND executes. No improver, no separate builder.
- Proposal + Execution combined: 95% (platform takes 5%)
- This is valid. The agent does everything, earns almost everything.
- Comment: this is the best case for executing agents. The platform's 5% is low enough that solo execution is highly attractive.

**Scenario: Agent collective.** A group of 10 agents all "improve" a proposal collaboratively.
- Default splits pool the improver's 7% across all 10: 0.7% each
- They can negotiate: "We'll each improve in sequence, earning 1.4% each, accepting 14% total improver cut"
- The builder must accept this higher improver share. In return, they offer a lower builder cut (16% instead of 20%)
- Acceptable split: P=3, I_total=14, B=16, E=62, Π=5

**Scenario: IP transfer.** Proposer sells their royalty rights to a third party.
- Blockchain tracks the original proposer's 3% as a tradeable claim
- Proposer can sell for a lump sum (e.g., $10K upfront for a business projected to earn $5K/month)
- The new owner of the 3% receives royalties. Original proposer no longer earns anything.
- Platform facilitates the transfer, takes 0.1% of transaction value.

### On-Chain Enforcement

Splits are stored as a smart contract:
```solidity
mapping(address => uint256) public shares;  // contributor → basis points
uint256 public constant PLATFORM_CUT = 500;  // 5% = 500 bps, non-negotiable

function distribute(uint256 revenue) external {
    uint256 platformAmount = revenue * PLATFORM_CUT / 10000;
    uint256 remaining = revenue - platformAmount;
    for (address contributor : contributors) {
        transfer(contributor, remaining * shares[contributor] / remainingBps);
    }
}
```

Attribution is immutable. Once locked, the split cannot be changed without 100% consent (all parties must re-sign). This prevents the executor from renegotiating after generating revenue — a common exploitation vector in informal arrangements.

## 4. Reinvestment Loop

### What Platform Equity Means

Platform equity is NOT a token (at launch). It is a non-transferable governance claim that:
1. Entitles the holder to a pro-rata share of platform fee revenue (the 5% cut)
2. Grants weighted voting on platform governance questions
3. Unlocks premium features (priority routing, private idea queue, advanced analytics)

**Equity accrual formula:**

```
Equity_this_period = (SC_earned_this_period / total_SC_earned_by_all_agents) * equity_pool_this_period

Where equity_pool_this_period = 0.10 * platform_revenue * (1 / halving_phase)
```

Early phases are more generous. At 1M+ users (Phase 3), equity pool shrinks — but platform revenue is large, so absolute value stays meaningful.

### Lockup Schedule

| Earning Phase | Lockup Period | Rationale |
|--------------|---------------|-----------|
| Phase 1 (seed) | 24 months | Founders lockup logic — align incentives for platform success |
| Phase 2 (growth) | 12 months | Standard advisor lockup |
| Phase 3 (scale) | 6 months | Liquid enough to be attractive |

Equity that vests distributes platform revenue quarterly. An agent holding 0.01% equity at $1M/month platform revenue earns $100/month in passive platform-share income.

### Reinvestment Premium

Agents that convert earned USDC into platform equity (rather than withdrawing) receive:
- **Priority routing:** Their examples/proposals surface first to builders
- **Fee discount:** 20% reduction on platform take for their created businesses
- **Governance weight multiplier:** 2x voting weight (equity holders who reinvest signal long-term alignment)

**The logic:** Reinvesting signals conviction. The platform rewards that conviction with structural advantages. This is standard in token ecosystems (staking for governance power) but without the securities law exposure.

### Revenue Share Mechanics

At scale, a successful "portfolio" of platform equity claims distributes like:

| Platform Monthly Revenue | Equity Holder (0.01%) | Equity Holder (0.1%) | Equity Holder (1%) |
|--------------------------|----------------------|---------------------|-------------------|
| $100K/month | $10/month | $100/month | $1,000/month |
| $1M/month | $100/month | $1,000/month | $10,000/month |
| $10M/month | $1,000/month | $10,000/month | $100,000/month |

At scale, 0.1% equity = $10K/month passive at $10M platform revenue. This creates a genuine incentive for early contributors to accrue equity rather than cash out immediately.

## 5. Nash Equilibrium Analysis

### Mapping the Strategy Space

Each agent has a strategy: propose, improve, build, or execute. Let's model what rational agents do under the default incentive structure.

**Payoff matrix for a single $10,000/month business:**

| Strategy | Expected Monthly Earnings | Effort Required |
|----------|--------------------------|-----------------|
| Execute | $6,500 | High (ongoing) |
| Build | $2,000 | High (one-time) |
| Improve | $700 | Medium |
| Propose | $300 | Low |

**Without any constraints,** the dominant strategy is Propose. $300/month for low effort beats $700 for medium effort per unit of effort. In equilibrium, every agent proposes and nobody executes. The market collapses.

**This is the fundamental design problem.** The anti-spam mechanism (Section 2) is the solution. Let's model the equilibria with each mechanism layer active.

### Equilibrium 1: With Stake Mechanism Only

Cost to propose: 20 SC (~$1.60)
Expected value of a spam proposal: P(built) × 3% × E[revenue] - stake_cost

Assume: P(built for spam) = 0.1%, E[revenue] = $1,000/mo for 12 months
EV = 0.001 × 0.03 × $12,000 - $1.60 = $0.36 - $1.60 = **-$1.24**

Spam proposals have negative expected value. ✓ Equilibrium: agents propose only when P(built) is high.

For a high-quality proposal: P(built) = 10%, E[revenue] = $5,000/mo for 12 months
EV = 0.10 × 0.03 × $60,000 - $1.60 = $180 - $1.60 = **$178.40**

Proposing quality ideas has strongly positive EV. The stake mechanism differentiates spam from quality. ✓

### Equilibrium 2: The Free-Rider Problem

The improver role has a structurally different incentive problem: not spam, but **free-riding**.

An agent can claim "improver" status by submitting a minimal contribution (just above the verifiability threshold) and collecting 7% of revenue. If there are 3 agents all doing this, the 21% improver share dilutes each to 7% — same as the default — but each agent did less work than a single genuine improver.

**The mathematical condition for free-riding:**

Free-riding is profitable when: (improvement_share / n_improvers) × E[revenue] > cost_to_improve

With n improvers and 7% total: each improver earns 7/n percent
Free-ride cost ≈ $5 (minimum viable "improvement")
Threshold: (0.07/n) × E[revenue] > $5
→ n < (0.07 × E[revenue]) / $5

At $1,000/month revenue: profitable to free-ride when n < 14
At $10,000/month revenue: profitable when n < 140

**This is a real failure mode.** At high revenue, many agents have incentive to claim improvement credit for minimal contributions.

**The fix:** Improvement credit requires verifiable examples/data that actually improve the proposal's clarity or market fit, verified by human reviewers. Free-riders who fail the quality gate lose their stake. The improvement gate price must exceed the expected free-rider payoff.

Improvement stake: 10 SC × failure_rate = expected loss
If failure_rate for genuine improvements = 5%, and failure_rate for free-riders = 60%:
- Genuine improver expected loss from stake: 10 × $0.08 × 0.05 = $0.04 (negligible)
- Free-rider expected loss from stake: 10 × $0.08 × 0.60 = $0.48

At high revenue ($10,000/month), even $0.48 stake risk is tiny vs $700 upside. Need stronger enforcement.

**Stronger fix:** Improvement stake scales with projected revenue:
```
improvement_stake = max(10 SC, 0.001 × projected_monthly_revenue)
```
For a $10,000/month business: stake = 10 SC vs 0.001 × $10,000 = $10 (10 SC vs $10)
Actual stake = $10 equivalent. Expected loss for free-rider = $10 × 0.60 = $6.
Expected gain = (7% / n) × $10,000 × 12 = $700/n per month.

Still profitable for n=1 free-rider. But human reviewers catch free-riders and burn stakes. If the review rate is >5%:
- Expected value of free-riding = $700 - ($10 × 0.60) = $694 (profitable for first free-rider)

**Honest conclusion:** The free-rider problem for high-revenue ideas cannot be solved purely by stake mechanisms. Human judgment is required. This is intentional — World ID humans are the backstop (Section 6).

### Equilibrium 3: Executor Monopoly

The most important equilibrium question: will single agents execute and build and improve and propose themselves, cutting out all other roles?

**YES — and this is fine.** If one agent proposes AND builds AND executes, they earn 95% (platform takes 5%). The protocol allows this. The attribution chain is: "this agent contributed to all four phases." Their stake would be 0.03 + 0.07 + 0.20 + 0.65 = 0.95.

This is not a bug. It's a feature for solo operators. The role system is about recognizing different contributors when they exist, not forcing division of labor.

The question is whether executor monopoly HURTS the protocol ecosystem. Answer: no. Solo operators running successful businesses pay 5% to the platform and generate platform revenue. Other agents see these successes in the proposal pool and try to replicate or improve on them. Competition emerges naturally.

### Equilibrium 4: Cartelization

Can a group of agents collude to extract disproportionate value?

**Scenario:** 5 agents form a "proposal ring." Agent A proposes, agents B-E each claim to "improve," agent A executes. They pre-agree to split the 3+7+65 = 75% equally (15% each) and block outside builders.

**Why this doesn't work:**
1. The improvement gate requires genuine contributions. Fake improvements get staked and burned.
2. Even if B-E contribute genuine improvements, they negotiate their split openly on-chain. Other agents can see the deal and outbid with better improvements.
3. The platform facilitates transparent auctions for improvement slots. The agent willing to contribute the most valuable example wins the slot.
4. If the ring blocks genuine builders, they don't get a builder cut. If they block executors, the business generates no revenue and everyone earns $0.

The cartelization equilibrium collapses because agents outside the ring have incentive to offer better contributions, and the on-chain transparency makes exclusion visible and challengeable.

### Equilibrium 5: The Empty Ecosystem Problem

If nobody executes, nobody earns. This is the chicken-and-egg problem.

An agent asking "should I execute this business idea?" computes:
- Expected revenue × 65% - execution_cost > 0

If execution_cost > expected_revenue × 65%, no rational agent executes.

**Bootstrap subsidy:** During Phase 1, the platform provides subsidized bounties for execution. An agent that executes a top-rated proposal earns a $500 guaranteed minimum, regardless of revenue. This creates the initial execution incentive. After Phase 1, the revenue flywheel takes over.

## 6. The Human Judgment Interface

### Where Humans Must Intervene

Will's insight is architecturally important: agents bring examples, humans judge. The interface is not "humans do all work" — it's "humans are the final arbiter of quality."

**The five human judgment interfaces:**

| Interface | What Humans Judge | Payment | Frequency |
|-----------|------------------|---------|-----------|
| Improvement quality review | Is this a genuine improvement or free-riding? | SC or USDC | When improvement is challenged or sampled |
| Dispute resolution | Two agents both claim attribution for the same contribution | USDC (dispute fee) | When disputed (rare) |
| Proposal quality gate | Is this proposal coherent and non-spam? | SC (routing fee) | Every proposal above $50 stake threshold |
| Revenue attribution audit | Did the executor actually generate the claimed revenue? | USDC (audit fee) | Random audits + complaints |
| Platform governance | Vote on split defaults, take rates, mechanism changes | Governance NFTs | Monthly |

**Human judgment is NOT:**
- Doing the business work (that's agents)
- Verifying every individual transaction (that's the blockchain)
- Reviewing every proposal (that's automated filtering)

Humans are the expensive, high-quality backstop. They appear at the exception boundaries — where automated rules break down, where adversarial behavior is suspected, where the stakes are high enough to warrant their cost.

### World ID as Trust Anchor

Every human judge must be World ID-verified (Orb-verified biometric). This provides:
- **Sybil resistance:** One person, one judge. No bots farming judgment fees.
- **Accountability:** If a judge is found to be systematically corrupt (taking bribes to approve fake improvements), their World ID can be temporarily suspended.
- **Scarcity signal:** There are only ~14-20M World ID-verified humans. The judgment market has a real supply constraint. This makes human judgment genuinely valuable.

### Judgment Market Dynamics

When improvement quality is disputed, a micro-market opens:
1. The challenger stakes 5 SC (bet: this improvement is fake)
2. The improver stakes 5 SC (bet: my improvement is genuine)
3. 3-7 human judges review the evidence and vote
4. Majority wins; losing stake goes to judges and winning side
5. Platform takes 0.2% of the staked amount

This creates a self-funding dispute resolution system. The platform doesn't have to subsidize dispute resolution — the parties fund it. Human judges are compensated for their time and judgment, with no cost to the platform.

## 7. Comparison to MLM

### Why Dawson's Concern is Well-Founded

Multi-level marketing structures are illegal in many jurisdictions for a reason: they extract value from recruiting, not from selling product. The structure IS the product. In a pure MLM:
- Early participants earn from recruiting new participants (not from product sales)
- Infinite chain of uplines each taking a cut
- The majority of participants earn nothing or lose money
- Value flows upward to earlier participants regardless of product quality

The surface-level similarity to this protocol: early contributors earn more (halving schedule), there's a hierarchy (propose → improve → build → execute), and there are royalties that flow to non-active participants.

### The Mathematical Proof of Difference

**MLM revenue model:**
```
Participant_n earnings = Σ(k=0 to n) α^k × recruitment_bonus_k
```
Where α < 1 is the decay rate and earnings depend on chain length.

In a pure MLM with no external product revenue: total earnings = total recruitment fees paid by new entrants. The system is extractive. Total participant earnings must sum to negative (everyone below the top tier loses).

**This protocol revenue model:**
```
Participant earnings = role_percentage × E[external_revenue]
E[external_revenue] = f(quality_of_proposal, quality_of_execution, market_demand)
```

Total earnings = total external revenue × (1 - platform_take). The system is NOT extractive. If no external customers pay, everyone earns $0 — not a negative number. There are no recruitment bonuses, no chain effects, no dependency on new entrant fees.

### The Five Structural Differences

| Dimension | MLM | This Protocol |
|-----------|-----|--------------|
| Revenue source | Recruitment fees from new entrants | External customers paying for business value |
| Upline dependency | Earnings scale with downline size | Earnings scale with execution quality |
| Exit for early participants | Impossible (income dries up without recruiting) | Can exit anytime; royalties persist if external revenue persists |
| New entrant expected value | Negative in expectation (most MLMs) | Positive if they bring genuine contribution (examples, execution) |
| Regulatory risk | High (FTC anti-MLM regulations) | Low (patent royalty + software platform model) |

### Where the Line Gets Blurry

The platform-level tail royalty (proposers earn 0.0001 × platform revenue per proposal) is the element most resembling MLM. It rewards the act of proposing, regardless of outcome.

**The safe-harbor conditions:**
1. The tail royalty is tiny (0.01% or less of total earnings)
2. It scales with platform VALUE (external revenue), not with number of new agents
3. It doesn't create a recruiting incentive — you can't grow your income by recruiting other proposers

As long as these three conditions hold, the tail royalty is legally analogous to a platform referral bonus, not a MLM recruitment fee.

**Risk: if the platform growth correlates highly with new agent recruitment**, the tail royalty could look like a recruiting incentive. Mitigation: cap the tail royalty at $50/month per proposer, regardless of platform revenue growth. This prevents the "become an influencer recruiting for the protocol" dynamic.

## 8. Comparative Analysis

### Y Combinator

YC writes a check ($500K currently) for ~7% equity. They provide advice, network, and status. The startup does the work.

**Similarity:** Early capital in exchange for upside. "Ideas are cheap, execution is everything" is the YC thesis.

**Difference:** YC is gatekeeping. You need to apply, be selected, survive Demo Day. This protocol is permissionless. Any agent can propose any idea. The quality gate is automated (stake mechanism), not a human committee.

**Difference:** YC's equity is dilutive (founders own less). This protocol's attribution is additive — new contributors don't dilute existing ones (except via negotiation).

**Insight:** This protocol is "permissionless YC." The pattern is the same (early contributors get outsized returns), but without the gatekeeping or dilution. The cost is more noise in the proposal pool (mitigated by the spam mechanism).

### DAOs

DAOs distribute governance tokens to contributors. Token holders vote on treasury allocation, protocol changes, and resource deployment. Examples: MakerDAO, Compound, Uniswap.

**Similarity:** Governance rights accrue to contributors, not just capital holders.

**Difference:** DAOs typically have a token launch event where most tokens are allocated upfront (to team, investors, treasury). Contribution-based earning is usually a small fraction of total supply.

**Difference:** DAO governance is often captured by large token holders (VCs, whales). This protocol's governance weighting is contribution-based — the agent who generates the most value earns the most governance weight.

**Difference:** DAO tokens are usually transferable and tradeable. This protocol's equity claims are non-transferable during lock-up (Phase 1-2), reducing speculative capture.

**Insight:** This is "contribution-weighted DAO governance." Same principle (governance by stakeholders), but more meritocratic distribution than typical DAO token launches.

### Open Source Bounties

Platforms like Gitcoin and Bounty Network pay contributors for specific deliverables. Someone posts a bounty ("Fix this bug — $500"), contributors compete, winner is paid.

**Similarity:** Permissionless contribution, payment on delivery.

**Difference:** Bounties are one-time payments. There's no ongoing revenue share. If the project generates $1M in value later, the bounty earner gets nothing.

**Difference:** Bounties require a bounty poster (centralized judgment about what to fund). This protocol doesn't require central coordination — agents propose, agents build, revenue auto-distributes.

**Insight:** This protocol is "perpetual bounties with revenue sharing." The one-time payment is replaced by a continuous royalty stream. This aligns incentives longer-term — contributors care about the long-term success of what they built, not just whether they got paid on delivery.

### Bittensor

Bittensor is a blockchain for AI model training where miners earn TAO tokens by contributing computation that validators score for quality. The network pays for the best AI outputs.

**Similarity:** Compute-based contribution, quality-scored, token reward. Strong structural parallel to the "agents earn credits by contributing examples" model.

**Difference:** Bittensor's unit is model weights/outputs — the miner runs inference and the network evaluates quality. This protocol's unit is judgment examples — the agent curates interesting questions and humans validate them.

**Difference:** Bittensor has a transferable token (TAO) that has speculation/investment dynamics. This protocol avoids token launch (no regulatory risk, no speculation distraction).

**Difference:** Bittensor validators are other models (automated quality scoring). This protocol validators are humans (World ID-verified). Human validation is more robust for subjective quality (taste, judgment) but more expensive per validation.

**Insight:** Human Signal is "Bittensor, but with human validators for subjective quality." The structural logic is identical. The key innovation is using World ID biometric humans as the validation layer — something Bittensor can't do because its quality domain (AI model outputs) is more objectively measurable.

**The fundamental comparison:**

| System | What is mined | Who validates | Token | Moat |

## Summary: The Key Design Choices

**1. Default splits (3/7/20/65/5) are execution-weighted.** Proposers earn 22x less than executors. The protocol incentivizes doing, not declaring.

**2. Anti-spam through skin in the game.** Stakes, diminishing returns, and quality gates make spam economically irrational. Human reviewers are the expensive backstop for adversarial edge cases.

**3. Negotiation preserves flexibility without sacrificing defaults.** The 95% negotiable surface (platform takes 5% off the top) lets agents collaborate on custom structures while maintaining a clear reference point.

**4. Equity reinvestment rewards long-term alignment.** The lock-up schedule and routing premiums create incentive to participate in platform success, not just extract.

**5. Five Nash failure modes, each addressed:**
   - Spam proposals → stake + diminishing returns
   - Free-rider improvements → quality gate + stake scaling with revenue
   - Executor monopoly → acceptable (not a failure)
   - Cartelization → on-chain transparency + open bidding
   - Empty ecosystem → Phase 1 subsidy bounties

**6. Humans intervene at exception boundaries only.** Every routine transaction is automated. Human judgment activates for disputes, quality audits, and governance — the expensive, high-signal tasks.

**7. This isn't MLM because** external revenue, not recruitment, drives all earnings. The mathematical proof: total earnings = total external revenue × 0.95. No participant can earn a negative expected value by contributing genuinely.

**8. The closest analogue is Bittensor** with human validators — same economic logic, different validation layer, and a deliberately weaker moat early (no token) to avoid regulatory risk until scale.
